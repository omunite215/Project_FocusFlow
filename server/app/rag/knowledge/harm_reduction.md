# Harm Reduction Guide

**IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER**: FocusFlow provides educational information to support student wellbeing. This is not medical advice. If you are in crisis, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) or your campus counseling center.

## Study Drug Misuse Among College Students

### The Reality
- Approximately 15-35% of college students have used prescription stimulants non-medically.
- Students with ADHD are sometimes pressured to share their medications.
- Non-prescribed stimulant use carries significant health and legal risks.
- The perceived benefit ("it helps me focus") is often exaggerated — studies show non-prescribed stimulants primarily increase confidence and wakefulness, not actual learning quality.

### Risks of Non-Prescribed Stimulant Use
- **Cardiovascular**: Increased heart rate, blood pressure, risk of cardiac events (especially with pre-existing conditions).
- **Psychological**: Anxiety, paranoia, psychotic episodes at high doses.
- **Dependency**: Tolerance develops quickly when used without medical supervision.
- **Legal**: Possession of someone else's Schedule II controlled substance is a felony in most states.
- **Academic**: Most universities consider non-prescribed stimulant use an academic integrity violation.

### Harm Reduction for Students Already Using
If a student is already using non-prescribed stimulants, shaming them will not help. Evidence-based harm reduction includes:
- Never combine stimulants with alcohol (masks intoxication, increases cardiac risk).
- Never take more than one dose in 24 hours.
- Stay hydrated — stimulants are dehydrating and increase body temperature.
- Eat before taking — reduces nausea and crash severity.
- Do not use if you have a heart condition, high blood pressure, or anxiety disorder.
- Recognize that the "productive feeling" does not equal actual learning. Test yourself on the material sober.

## Caffeine and ADHD Medications

### Interactions and Risks
- Caffeine is a mild stimulant that affects the same neurotransmitter pathways as ADHD medications.
- Combining caffeine with prescription stimulants compounds cardiovascular effects (heart rate, blood pressure).
- Many ADHD students use caffeine as self-medication before diagnosis. After starting prescription stimulants, caffeine intake should be reassessed.

### Safe Caffeine Guidelines with ADHD Medications
- **Maximum recommended**: 200mg caffeine per day when taking stimulant medications (roughly 2 small cups of coffee).
- **Timing**: Avoid caffeine within 4 hours of taking stimulant medication — the combined peak can cause jitteriness, anxiety, and racing heart.
- **Signs of too much**: Racing heart, trembling hands, inability to sit still, anxious thoughts, difficulty sleeping.
- **Better alternatives**: Green tea (lower caffeine, L-theanine provides calming effect), cold water (hydration improves alertness), brief exercise (10-minute walk boosts focus for 1-2 hours).

## Sleep and ADHD

### Why Sleep Matters More for ADHD Brains
- Sleep deprivation worsens every ADHD symptom: inattention, impulsivity, emotional dysregulation.
- One night of poor sleep (less than 6 hours) can reduce next-day focus by 30-50%.
- Stimulant medications are less effective when sleep-deprived — the brain doesn't have enough baseline function to enhance.
- Chronic sleep deprivation mimics ADHD symptoms even in neurotypical brains.

### Sleep Hygiene for ADHD Students
- **Consistent schedule**: Same bedtime and wake time, even on weekends (within 1 hour).
- **Stimulant timing**: Take stimulants early enough that they wear off by bedtime. Discuss timing with your doctor.
- **Screen curfew**: Blue light suppresses melatonin. Stop screens 30-60 minutes before bed, or use blue light filters.
- **No caffeine after 2pm**: Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. An afternoon coffee at 3pm still has half its effect at 9pm.
- **Wind-down routine**: ADHD brains need external cues to transition to sleep mode. A consistent routine (dim lights, read, stretch) signals the body.

### The All-Nighter Myth
- Students who pull all-nighters score an average of 5-10% LOWER than those who sleep.
- Learning consolidation happens during sleep — studying all night without sleep means the material doesn't transfer to long-term memory.
- Better strategy: Study until 11pm, sleep 7 hours, review for 30 minutes in the morning. This produces better retention than studying until 3am.

## Stress and Burnout

### Academic Burnout in ADHD Students
ADHD students are at higher risk for academic burnout because:
- They expend more energy on tasks that come easily to neurotypical peers.
- The constant effort of self-regulation is exhausting.
- Repeated "failures" (missed deadlines, forgotten assignments) erode self-efficacy.
- Masking ADHD symptoms in academic settings is cognitively draining.

### Signs of Burnout
- Studying feels physically painful or impossible, not just boring.
- Emotional numbness or apathy toward grades and deadlines.
- Physical symptoms: chronic headache, stomach issues, muscle tension.
- Increased reliance on caffeine, stimulants, or substances to function.
- Social withdrawal and irritability.

### Burnout Prevention Strategies
- **Set hard stop times**: Decide when studying ends BEFORE starting. Honor the limit.
- **Celebrate small wins**: Studied for 20 minutes? That counts. Showing up matters.
- **Build in recovery days**: One day per week with zero academic obligations.
- **Seek support**: Campus counseling, ADHD coaching, peer study groups.
- **Recognize the pattern**: If focus drops below 2/5 for an entire session, it's a rest day signal, not a failure.

## Substance Use Considerations

### Alcohol
- Alcohol impairs memory consolidation even in small amounts.
- Studying while hungover is essentially wasted time — retention drops dramatically.
- ADHD medications and alcohol should never be combined (increased risk of alcohol poisoning, cardiac events).

### Cannabis
- THC impairs working memory and attention for 24-72 hours after use, even when no longer feeling "high."
- CBD alone has not been shown to improve or worsen ADHD symptoms.
- Students using cannabis regularly may notice worsening ADHD symptoms that improve with cessation.

### The Bottom Line
FocusFlow does not judge or stigmatize substance use. The goal is to provide accurate information so students can make informed decisions about how their choices affect their academic performance and cognitive function.
