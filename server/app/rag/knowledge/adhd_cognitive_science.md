# ADHD Cognitive Science

## Executive Function and ADHD

Executive function refers to the set of mental skills that help us plan, focus attention, remember instructions, and juggle multiple tasks. ADHD impairs executive function in several key ways:

- **Working memory deficits**: Difficulty holding information in mind while using it. Students may read a paragraph and forget the beginning by the end.
- **Inhibition difficulties**: Trouble suppressing distracting thoughts or impulses. A student might switch from studying to social media without conscious decision.
- **Cognitive flexibility challenges**: Difficulty shifting between tasks or mental sets. Transitioning from math to essay writing can feel overwhelming.
- **Planning and organization deficits**: Trouble breaking large tasks into steps and sequencing them. A 10-page paper feels like a monolithic, impossible task.

## Dopamine and the ADHD Brain

ADHD is fundamentally a disorder of dopamine regulation, not a lack of intelligence or willpower:

- The ADHD brain has lower baseline dopamine levels in the prefrontal cortex.
- This creates a constant search for stimulation — the brain needs more dopamine to activate the reward system.
- High-interest tasks produce enough dopamine for focus (hyperfocus). Low-interest tasks do not, making sustained attention extremely difficult.
- Stimulant medications work by increasing dopamine availability in the prefrontal cortex, narrowing the gap between baseline and threshold.

## Time Blindness

Time blindness is a core ADHD symptom, not a character flaw:

- The ADHD brain perceives time in two modes: "now" and "not now." There is no gradient between 5 minutes and 5 hours.
- Students with ADHD consistently underestimate how long tasks will take by 30-50%.
- A "5-minute break" can become 2 hours because the brain cannot perceive the passage of time.
- External time cues (timers, check-ins, visual countdowns) are essential compensatory tools.
- Frequent, gentle time check-ins help anchor the student in the present moment.

## Hyperfocus: The Double-Edged Sword

Hyperfocus is not a superpower — it's dysregulated attention:

- ADHD brains can lock onto high-stimulation tasks for hours, ignoring hunger, time, and other responsibilities.
- Students may spend 4 hours on one subject while ignoring three others with closer deadlines.
- The key is not to eliminate hyperfocus but to channel it toward the right task at the right time.
- Session planning should account for hyperfocus by scheduling the most important subject during peak focus windows.

## Cognitive Fatigue in ADHD

Students with ADHD experience cognitive fatigue differently:

- Mental energy depletes faster due to the constant effort of self-regulation.
- A neurotypical student might sustain focus for 45-60 minutes. An ADHD student may hit fatigue at 15-25 minutes.
- Pushing through fatigue produces diminishing returns — focus quality drops exponentially, not linearly.
- Optimal study block length for ADHD students: 15-30 minutes with 5-10 minute breaks.
- Movement breaks (walking, stretching, jumping jacks) are more restorative than passive breaks (scrolling phone).
- Signs of cognitive fatigue: re-reading the same paragraph, losing track of what was just studied, increasing fidgeting, emotional frustration.

## ADHD Subtypes and Study Implications

### Inattentive Type
- Primary challenge: sustaining attention on non-preferred tasks.
- Benefits from: frequent task switching, environmental novelty, external accountability.
- Study strategy: rotate subjects every 20-25 minutes, use active recall over passive reading.

### Hyperactive-Impulsive Type
- Primary challenge: sitting still and resisting impulses to switch tasks.
- Benefits from: movement integration, fidget tools, standing desks.
- Study strategy: incorporate physical movement between blocks, use body doubling techniques.

### Combined Type
- Experiences both inattention and hyperactivity challenges.
- Benefits from: highly structured sessions with built-in variety and movement.
- Study strategy: short blocks (15-20 min), alternating seated and active study methods.

## Working Memory and Study Techniques

The average working memory capacity is 4 items (plus or minus 1). For ADHD students, this is often on the lower end:

- **Chunking**: Break information into groups of 3-4 items maximum.
- **Active recall**: Testing yourself is 3x more effective than re-reading because it strengthens retrieval pathways.
- **Spaced repetition**: Review material at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days).
- **Dual coding**: Combine verbal and visual information. Draw diagrams while reading text.
- **External storage**: Write everything down. Don't rely on memory for task lists, deadlines, or study plans.

## Emotional Regulation and ADHD

ADHD includes significant emotional dysregulation that affects studying:

- Frustration threshold is lower — students may abandon difficult material faster.
- Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) can make academic feedback feel crushing.
- Study sessions that feel unproductive can trigger shame spirals.
- AI coaching should always frame progress positively and celebrate effort, not just outcomes.
- Never use guilt, shame, or comparison to motivate. This backfires severely with ADHD brains.
