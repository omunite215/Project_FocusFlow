# ADHD Medication Reference

**IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER**: This information is for educational purposes only. Always consult your prescribing physician for medical advice. FocusFlow does not provide medical recommendations — only general information to help you understand how medication timing relates to study performance.

## Stimulant Medications

### Methylphenidate-Based

#### Ritalin (Immediate Release)
- **Onset**: 20-30 minutes after taking.
- **Peak effect**: 1-2 hours after taking.
- **Duration**: 3-4 hours total.
- **Study optimization**: Start studying 20-30 minutes after dose. Schedule hardest material during the 1-2 hour peak window.
- **Common side effects**: Decreased appetite, difficulty sleeping if taken late, mild headache.

#### Concerta (Extended Release)
- **Onset**: 30-60 minutes after taking.
- **Peak effect**: Initial peak at 1-2 hours, sustained through 6-8 hours with ascending dose profile.
- **Duration**: 10-12 hours total.
- **Study optimization**: Take in the morning. Focus window is broad — plan demanding subjects in the first 6-8 hours. Lighter review in the afternoon as effect tapers.
- **Common side effects**: Decreased appetite, insomnia if taken after 10am, stomach discomfort.

#### Focalin (Dexmethylphenidate)
- **Onset**: 30 minutes after taking.
- **Peak effect**: 1-1.5 hours (IR), sustained 4-6 hours (XR).
- **Duration**: 4-5 hours (IR), 8-12 hours (XR).
- **Study optimization**: Similar to Ritalin but often reported as smoother. Schedule intense work during peak window.

### Amphetamine-Based

#### Adderall (Mixed Amphetamine Salts)
- **Onset**: 30-60 minutes after taking.
- **Peak effect**: 1-3 hours (IR), 4-7 hours (XR).
- **Duration**: 4-6 hours (IR), 10-12 hours (XR).
- **Study optimization**: For IR, plan two study sessions timed with each dose. For XR, leverage the broad peak for extended study sessions.
- **Common side effects**: Decreased appetite, dry mouth, increased heart rate, difficulty sleeping.

#### Vyvanse (Lisdexamfetamine)
- **Onset**: 1-2 hours after taking (requires metabolic conversion).
- **Peak effect**: 3-4 hours after taking.
- **Duration**: 10-14 hours total (longest among stimulants).
- **Study optimization**: Take early in the morning. The slow onset means you may need 1-2 hours before peak focus. Plan hardest subjects for mid-morning to early afternoon. Excellent for long study sessions.
- **Common side effects**: Decreased appetite (often strongest), dry mouth, potential afternoon crash.

### Key Stimulant Principles for Study Timing

1. **Never study during the trough**: The period before a dose kicks in or after it wears off is the worst time for demanding material.
2. **Align difficulty with medication peak**: Schedule the hardest, least interesting subject during peak medication effect.
3. **Account for the crash**: Most stimulants have a period of below-baseline focus as they wear off. Plan easy, enjoyable tasks for this window.
4. **Eating affects absorption**: Taking stimulants with high-fat meals can delay onset. Taking on an empty stomach speeds onset but may increase side effects.
5. **Hydration matters**: Stimulants are dehydrating. Dehydration worsens focus. Drink water throughout study sessions.

## Non-Stimulant Medications

### Strattera (Atomoxetine)
- **Onset**: 2-4 weeks for full therapeutic effect (not immediate like stimulants).
- **Duration**: 24-hour coverage (taken daily).
- **Study optimization**: Since it provides baseline improvement rather than peaks, study timing is less critical. Focus on general energy patterns instead.
- **Common side effects**: Nausea, decreased appetite, fatigue (usually improves after 2-4 weeks).

### Wellbutrin (Bupropion)
- **Onset**: 2-4 weeks for full effect.
- **Duration**: 24-hour coverage.
- **Study optimization**: Provides mild stimulant-like effect. Study timing should follow natural energy patterns rather than medication timing.
- **Common side effects**: Dry mouth, insomnia, headache.

### Intuniv (Guanfacine)
- **Onset**: 1-2 weeks for noticeable effect.
- **Duration**: 24-hour coverage.
- **Study optimization**: Primarily helps with emotional regulation and impulse control rather than focus directly. Can reduce the anxiety that interferes with studying.
- **Common side effects**: Drowsiness (especially initially), low blood pressure, fatigue.

## Medication and Study Session Planning

### How to Factor Medication into Study Plans

1. **Know your medication's timeline**: Record when you take your medication and when you feel it kick in, peak, and wear off.
2. **Track the pattern**: Over 1-2 weeks, rate your focus at different times. You'll see a clear pattern related to medication timing.
3. **Plan around peaks**: Schedule the most cognitively demanding work during your medication's peak window.
4. **Plan around troughs**: Use medication trough periods for low-demand tasks: organizing notes, light review, administrative tasks.
5. **Don't force it**: If your medication has worn off and you can't focus, it's better to stop and rest than to push through with zero retention.

### Medication Timing Log

Students should track:
- Time medication was taken
- Time focus improved (onset)
- Time of peak focus
- Time focus started declining
- Time medication fully wore off

This data allows FocusFlow to personalize session plans based on the student's actual medication response, not textbook averages.

## Important Safety Notes

- Never adjust dosage without consulting your physician.
- Never share prescribed medications with others.
- Store medications securely — stimulant medications are controlled substances.
- If you experience chest pain, severe anxiety, or unusual symptoms, contact your healthcare provider immediately.
- Tolerance can develop over time — if your medication feels less effective, discuss with your doctor rather than self-adjusting.
